Outcome 1.4 - Describe the risks that might impact digital projects


There are various risks associated to digital projects that may result in minor to significant detrimental effects on the project and/or to the business. These risks could include cyber security. To illustrate, if an IT user is connected to the wifi or even their company’s Ethernet, it is possible for small to large scale cyber-attacks. These attacks either prevent the user from accessing their information, delete their information or affect the manner in which the user may use their information. Consequently, this risk may impose a delay or increased cost to the project. There may also be risks presented due to the hardware used, accessibility and/or quality of the internet connection which could affect the ability to communicate, research or develop a project. To combat these potential problems, one could invest in equipment such as an external hard-drive to free memory on older devices, upload data to cloud storage and backup files on backup external hard-drive(s). The risks that might impact digital projects may include problems with hardware, software and expertise of the individual. To illustrate if a relevant party encounters a problem implementing their objective due to difficulties with their resources or ability then competitors may develop the same idea first or the significant delays may affect relations with various stakeholders and/or shareholders. Other risks may include not adapting one’s approach to each project or party, not being fully engaged with the concept or management of the project, being disorganised, missing deadlines and not having an appropriate plan for after the project has been delivered. Another important risk may be the management of personal data. If the project collects, stores, manages and/or uses customer data then they manner in which they do this will be regulated by the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).

Outcome 1.7 - Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects – How does GDPR impact digital projects?


The General Data Protection Regulation, also referred to as GDPR is a European Union legislation that regulates the processing of personal data. It came into effect on May 25, 2018 and superseded the previous EU directive passed in 1995 that regulated that area. One of the primary objectives of GDPR was to change the legal reulations regarding the processing and protecting personal data. These changes are designed to be better suited to the challenges presented by technological development and globilisation that have subsequently facilitated the availability of one's personal data and the opportunity for the pervasive use of personal data. Personal data may refer to any information that may identify an individual such as their name, age, location data, identification number. GDPR increases the scope of an individual user's rights and allows for data subjects e.g. users of an online service to possess greater control over the processing of their information. Therefore, this allows the user to possess a deeper insight into how the information concerning them is stored, how it is used and provides them with the ability to restrict or completely prohibit the processing of their data. Therefore, companies including companies focussing on digital projects are required to comply with the GDPR. Therefore the way a digital project processes, stores and handles data is regulated by GDPR. The image of a digital project may also be affected by GDPR too. For example, if the project fails to implement the necessary and appropriate security measures required to ensure the correct processing, storage and protection of data, then they increase the risk of a data breach. It may be compulsory that the communication of a data breach to the relevant supervisory body and the users affected by the breach may be required upon a data breach occuring. Therefore compliance to the GDPR is key. Companies may be penalised upon non-compliance with GDPR with the most severe punishments being an administrative fine of up to EUR 20,000,000 or up to 4% of the total worldwide annual turnover of the preceding financial year, whichever is higher. That may cause a real harm for both small and large-size companies. Other legal constraints and guidelines that may affect digital companies are the protection of one's intelluctual property and copyrighting any original work through the digital medium.
